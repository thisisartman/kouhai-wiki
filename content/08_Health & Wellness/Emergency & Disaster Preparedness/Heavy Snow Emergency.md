Minami-Uonuma is in Japan's snow country (雪国, *yukiguni*). In heavy winters, snowfall can exceed 3 metres accumulated on the ground. This is a genuine hazard, not just an inconvenience. Knowing how to handle it safely is part of living here.

---

## What "Heavy Snow" Looks Like Here

This isn't occasional dusting. In peak months (January–February):
- 30–60cm of new snowfall can fall overnight
- Roads become impassable or dangerous without snowploughs
- Roof loads become structurally significant
- Power lines can fail
- Visibility drops to near-zero in blizzard conditions

The local population is accustomed to this. Students from warmer climates often underestimate it.

---

## During a Heavy Snowfall

### Stay Inside If Possible
If a heavy snowstorm is forecast (check NHK World or Yahoo! Japan weather), avoid unnecessary travel. Roads and paths are dangerous. IUJ may announce class suspensions or travel advisories, so watch official channels.

### If You Must Go Out
- Wear proper snow boots with grip: smooth-soled shoes on ice will result in falls
- Move slowly, especially on slopes and stairways
- Stick to cleared paths where possible
- Tell someone where you're going and when you expect to be back

### Watch for Ice Under Snow
Fresh snow conceals compacted ice underneath: the invisible hazard. Particularly dangerous on inclines and near building overhangs.

## Roof Snow: Stay Clear of Buildings

**Serious hazard.** Snow accumulates on roofs and can slide off suddenly in large masses, particularly on sunny days after heavy snowfall as the snow begins to melt. This is called 落雪 (*rakusetsu*).

- **Never walk directly alongside a building wall when there's significant roof snow**
- Look for yellow warning signs (落雪注意, *rakusetsu chūi*): these mark danger zones
- IUJ buildings may have guards or nets but this is not universal
- Dorm Staff sometimes clear roof snow directly (dropping it to the ground). When this happens, IUJ notifies affected dorms in advance and asks everyone to stay away from windows and not look outside during the work
- Don't force open a window that's stuck from snow buildup between it and the AC unit outside (common in SD2/SD3, especially higher floors): you can break the screen. Contact Dorm Staff instead.

> ⚠️ Roof snow falls silently and fast. This is a genuine cause of injury and death in snow country every winter. Take the warning signs seriously.

## Snow Shovelling

Dorm residents may be expected to assist with snow clearing around the building. If asked:

- Use a plastic snow shovel (not metal on paved surfaces)
- Lift with your legs: snow is heavy, and back injuries from shovelling are common
- Do not overexert; take breaks
- Clear paths promptly after snowfall: packed snow becomes ice overnight

## Power Outages

Heavy snow can bring down power lines. If power goes out:

1. Check if it's building-wide or room-only (circuit breaker vs external outage)
2. Keep phones charged: conserve battery if outage is prolonged
3. Report to Dorm Staff (SD1 Dorm Staff Office): **025-779-1555 (ext. 555)**, dorm@iuj.ac.jp, business hours 8:30–17:00 (closed 12:00–13:00)
4. Dress in layers immediately: room temperatures drop fast without heating
5. IUJ should have an emergency protocol for extended outages; ask student affairs in advance

**Emergency heat:** If power is out and it's very cold, gather with others. A single person in a small space generates significant heat. Do not use outdoor-rated gas stoves or charcoal inside: carbon monoxide risk.

## Getting Stranded

If you're away from campus when a storm hits unexpectedly:
- Go to the nearest convenience store, mall, or station: they stay open
- Call the campus emergency number to inform someone of your situation
- Do not drive in whiteout conditions: pull off the road safely, keep the engine running for warmth, and crack a window to prevent CO buildup

## Winter Readiness Checklist

- [ ] Snow boots with grip in your room before December
- [ ] Warm layers accessible (not packed away)
- [ ] Power bank charged
- [ ] ¥5,000–10,000 cash (ATMs may be inaccessible in severe weather)
- [ ] Basic food supplies for 2–3 days (can be snowed in)
- [ ] IUJ emergency number saved in your phone
- [ ] Flashlight or phone torch working
