Japan is not a country that naturally accommodates dietary restrictions. Awareness is growing — especially in cities — but rural Niigata lags behind. This article is for anyone who needs to navigate halal requirements, gluten intolerance, or food allergies at IUJ.

---

## Halal

### The Situation in Rural Niigata
Certified halal restaurants near Urasa are extremely rare. The Muslim student community at IUJ has generally developed workarounds — self-cooking being the primary one.

### What to Avoid
- **Pork (豚肉, butaniku)** — widespread; in ramen broth, bento fillings, gyoza, many sauces
- **Alcohol in cooking** — mirin and sake are used in almost all Japanese cooking; technically haram. Many Muslim students make a personal judgment call on this.
- **Gelatin** — often pork-based in Japanese snacks and some desserts
- **Lard** — occasionally used in ramen broth and some baked goods

### What's Generally Safe
- Plain rice, vegetables, tofu, eggs (confirm egg use is acceptable for you)
- Sashimi/sushi of fish and seafood — fish is generally halal; avoid crab-stick (surimi) as it may contain additives
- Vegetarian dishes (minus dashi concerns — see [[Vegetarian & Vegan in Rural Niigata]])
- Fruit, plain nuts, dairy products

## Gluten-Free

### The Situation
Japan's diet is heavily wheat-based — soy sauce (contains wheat), ramen, udon, tempura batter, and countless processed foods all contain gluten. Celiac disease awareness is low among Japanese food service staff.

### What to Avoid
- Soy sauce (醤油) — almost universally contains wheat; use **tamari (たまり)** as substitute (most tamari is wheat-free; check label)
- Ramen, udon, soba (soba is buckwheat but often contains wheat — ask or buy 100% buckwheat)
- Tempura batter, breadcrumbs (panko)
- Most processed snacks and sauces

### What's Generally Safe
- Plain rice (Japan's staple — you're in luck)
- Most fresh vegetables and fruits
- Tofu — plain variety; check ingredient list on flavored types
- Plain meat and fish (unprocessed)
- Miso — most miso is gluten-free, but some varieties contain barley; check label
- Rice noodles (フォー, ビーフン)
- Pure soba (100% そば粉) — confirm it's not mixed with wheat

### Tamari as Soy Sauce Substitute
- Available at most supermarkets; look for **グルテンフリー** (gluten-free) on the label
- Kikkoman and Yamasa both make gluten-free tamari

### Useful Phrases

| Japanese | Romaji | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| グルテンアレルギーがあります | Guruten arerugī ga arimasu | I have a gluten allergy |
| 小麦粉が入っていますか？ | Komugiko ga haitte imasu ka? | Does this contain wheat flour? |
| 醤油は使っていますか？ | Shōyu wa tsukatte imasu ka? | Do you use soy sauce? |

## Food Allergies

Japan's labeling law requires disclosure of 8 major allergens on packaged foods:

| Allergen | Japanese |
|---|---|
| Wheat | 小麦 (こむぎ) |
| Buckwheat | そば |
| Eggs | 卵 (たまご) |
| Milk | 乳 (にゅう) |
| Peanuts | 落花生 (らっかせい) |
| Shrimp | えび |
| Crab | かに |
| Tree nuts (walnut, cashew) | くるみ / カシューナッツ |

Additional 20 recommended allergens include salmon, squid, sesame, soy, and others — disclosed voluntarily.

> 💡 Google Translate camera mode on ingredient lists is fast and reasonably accurate for allergen identification. Use it at the supermarket.

### At Restaurants
Japan's allergy disclosure at restaurants is inconsistent — unlike packaged food, restaurant meals have no legal labeling requirement. For serious allergies:
- Carry an **allergy card** in Japanese explaining your condition
- Printable allergy cards: [allergytranslation.com](https://www.allergytranslation.com) — free, professionally translated
- Communicate clearly; don't assume the kitchen understands cross-contamination risks

## General Strategies for Dietary Restrictions at IUJ

1. **Cook most of your meals** — the only way to guarantee full ingredient control. See [[Cooking Basics & Cheap Meal Prep]]
2. **Build a pantry of safe staples** — once you have your safe ingredients identified, stocking up makes daily cooking much lower friction
3. **Connect with others who share your restriction** — the IUJ community is international enough that you'll find others navigating the same challenges; share knowledge
4. **Inform the campus cafeteria** — ask if accommodations can be made; they may not advertise options that exist
5. **Online ordering** — Amazon Japan, iHerb, and specialty stores can fill gaps that local supermarkets can't
