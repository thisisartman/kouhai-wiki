## The Core Timeline Problem

Most IR students make the same mistake: **they treat the thesis as a Year 2 problem**. It isn't. By the time Year 2 starts, you should already have:

- A general topic area identified
- A supervisor relationship developing
- Some preliminary reading done

Students who start from scratch in Year 2 are always racing.

---

## Recommended Timeline

| Period | What Should Be Happening |
|---|---|
| **Fall (Year 1)** | Explore topic areas through coursework; note which professors' work interests you |
| **End of Fall / Winter (Year 1)** | Narrow to 2–3 possible topic areas; start attending thesis-relevant lectures |
| **Winter (Year 1)** | Approach potential supervisors informally; begin preliminary literature reading |
| **Spring (Year 1)** | Confirm supervisor; draft research question; submit thesis proposal if required |
| **Summer** | Literature review; research design; data collection begins |
| **Fall (Year 2)** | Writing begins in earnest; first draft to supervisor |
| **Winter (Year 2)** | Revisions; second draft; preparation for submission |
| **Spring (Year 2)** | Final submission; defense |

> ⚠️ Confirm exact IUJ thesis submission deadlines and defense schedule with the IR program office — these are hard deadlines.

## Choosing a Supervisor

This is the most consequential academic decision you make at IUJ. Get it right.

**What to consider:**
- **Research fit** — does their expertise align with your topic area? A supervisor working in your field gives better feedback and opens relevant networks
- **Availability and engagement** — some professors are more hands-on than others. Ask seniors directly about specific professors' supervision styles before committing.
- **Communication style** — you'll interact with this person regularly for 12–18 months. Compatibility matters.
- **Track record** — how have their past thesis students done? Did they finish on time? Did the work get published or recognized?

**How to approach a potential supervisor:**
1. Read 1–2 of their papers or recent work — come informed
2. Go to their office hours with a rough topic idea, not a finalized proposal
3. Ask if they'd be open to supervising work in that direction
4. It's okay to have exploratory conversations with multiple professors before committing

> 💡 Most professors appreciate students who show genuine intellectual interest in their field. The worst outcome of approaching someone is they say no or suggest another faculty member — not a disaster.

## Developing Your Research Question

A good thesis research question is:
- **Specific** — not "what causes conflict" but "why did X policy fail in Y context between Z years"
- **Answerable** — with the data and access you actually have
- **Original** — adds something to existing scholarship; doesn't just summarize
- **Arguable** — has a real answer that isn't obvious

Common early mistakes:
- Too broad — trying to solve a global problem in 80 pages
- Too descriptive — summarizing what happened instead of explaining why
- Data mismatch — proposing a quantitative study without access to the data

> 💡 Your research question will change multiple times. That's normal. Start with a direction, not a perfect question.

## Methodology Basics

Most IR theses use one of:

| Method | When to Use |
|---|---|
| **Qualitative case study** | Deep analysis of a specific event, country, or policy; rich contextual explanation |
| **Comparative case study** | Comparing 2–4 cases to identify patterns or test a theory |
| **Quantitative / statistical** | Testing a hypothesis across a large number of cases with data |
| **Mixed methods** | Combining quant and qual; ambitious but common in IR |
| **Discourse / content analysis** | Analyzing texts, speeches, media for patterns and meaning |

> 💡 Choose a method that matches your research question — don't pick a method and then find a question that fits it.

## Using Research Tools

- Start your literature review in [[Research Databases & Academic Tools]] early — JSTOR, UN Digital Library, ProQuest are your main sources
- Use **Zotero** from day one — retroactively organizing citations is painful
- Keep a running "argument map" or outline document — your thinking will drift without it

## Working with Your Supervisor

- **Set a meeting cadence early** — monthly at minimum; more frequently during writing phase
- **Send drafts before meetings** — don't show up empty-handed expecting direction
- **Be specific about what feedback you need** — "does this argument hold?" is more useful than "what do you think?"
- **Respond to feedback in writing** — send a brief email summarizing what you changed and why after each round of revisions
- **Don't disappear** — supervisors notice. Silence is interpreted as lack of progress.
