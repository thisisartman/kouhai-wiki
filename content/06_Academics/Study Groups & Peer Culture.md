IUJ's small cohort size is one of its biggest academic advantages, if you use it right. The peer learning environment here is genuinely stronger than larger universities, but it requires you to actively participate.

---

## Why Study Groups Matter at IUJ

- Class sizes are small: professors notice engagement (and disengagement)
- Many courses are discussion and case-based: preparation is collective, not just individual
- Your classmates come from 30+ countries with vastly different professional backgrounds: this is a resource, not background noise
- Group assignments are common; informal study groups make formal group work less painful

---

## How Study Groups Typically Form

- Organically after class: someone suggests continuing a discussion over coffee
- Via LINE: most course-level group chats evolve into informal study spaces
- Library group rooms: the default venue for structured study sessions
- Dorm common rooms: more casual, late-night option

> 💡 Don't wait for someone else to organize it. If you want a study group, propose it in the class LINE chat. Most people will join. They just don't want to be the one to ask.

---

## Peer Learning Culture at IUJ

**What works:**
- Sharing notes and readings across cohort is standard and expected
- Pre-class discussion groups to process dense readings are common
- Peer feedback on drafts before submission: useful and widely practiced
- Seniors sharing past syllabi, readings, and tips with juniors: this wiki is part of that tradition

**What to avoid:**
- Splitting assignments and submitting without individual understanding: academic integrity risk
- Over-relying on one "smart" person in the group: check IUJ's academic honesty policy
- Insular nationality-based study groups: you lose the entire point of an international cohort

## Cross-Cultural Dynamics in Group Work

IUJ group work surfaces real cultural differences in communication styles, work pace, and expectations. Some patterns that come up:

- **Directness vs. indirectness**: some cultures give feedback bluntly; others soften it heavily. Neither is wrong, both need calibration.
- **Leadership assumption**: whoever speaks first or loudest isn't always leading; rotate facilitation
- **Time orientation**: "let's meet at 7pm" means different things to different people. Set deadlines explicitly.
- **Free riders**: they exist everywhere. Address it early within the group; involve the professor only as a last resort.

> 💡 A 10-minute "working norms" conversation at the start of any group project prevents 80% of group conflict. Set expectations about deadlines, communication channels, and how disagreements get resolved.

## Connecting with Japanese Students

Japanese students at IUJ are often quieter in class settings but highly engaged one-on-one. Some context:
- Many are experienced professionals returning for graduate study: their insights in class discussions are worth drawing out
- Language exchange is a genuine mutual interest: most want English practice; you might want Japanese practice. See [[Japanese Language Courses at IUJ]]
- Direct social invitations work better than open-ended "let's hang out sometime": suggest a specific activity

## Faculty Office Hours

Underused by most students. IUJ professors are accessible in ways that wouldn't be possible at a larger university:
- Use office hours to discuss paper topics before committing to them
- Follow up on class discussions you found interesting
- Build relationships early: especially important for IR students who need thesis supervisors. See [[Thesis Guide (IR) — Supervisor, Timeline, Tips]]
