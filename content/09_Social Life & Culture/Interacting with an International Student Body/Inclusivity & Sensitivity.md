IUJ brings together people from countries that are, in some cases, in active geopolitical conflict. People with deeply different religious practices, dietary restrictions, political histories, and social norms share a 300-person campus for two years. This creates remarkable richness, and occasional friction. Navigating it well is a skill worth developing.

---

## The Baseline

You will encounter viewpoints, practices, and histories that are unfamiliar, uncomfortable, or directly at odds with your own. This is the deal. The IUJ experience is substantially about learning to hold this without either performing tolerance or losing your own perspective.

**What's expected:** Genuine curiosity and basic respect.
**What's not expected:** Agreement with everything or the suppression of your own views.

---

## Common Areas of Tension

### National / Political Identity
Students from countries with contested borders, historical conflicts, or ongoing geopolitical disputes will sometimes encounter people on the other side of those disputes. IUJ's academic culture generally keeps this in the realm of intellectual debate, but it gets personal.

- Distinguish between a political position and a personal attack
- "I disagree with your government's position" is not the same as "I disrespect you"
- Engage with curiosity rather than defensiveness where possible

### Religious Practice
IUJ has students with a wide range of religious observances, including daily prayer schedules, dietary restrictions, dress practices, and restrictions on certain social activities (alcohol, mixed-gender spaces, etc.).

- Don't pressure people to participate in things their religion restricts
- Don't treat religious practice as an curiosity or inconvenience
- Ask, don't assume. "Do you drink?" is a better opener than offering someone alcohol and reading their decline as awkward.

### Gender and Sexuality
LGBTQ+ students are part of the IUJ community. Japan's social environment around sexuality is complex — not hostile in the same way as some other countries, but not openly affirming either.

- Avoid assumptions about partners, relationship structures, or gender
- Be matter-of-fact about your own expectations; let others be the same

### Caste, Class, Race
These are present at IUJ even when they go undiscussed. Students from societies with strong hierarchies sometimes replicate them unconsciously. Be aware of what assumptions you're importing.

## Being Called Out

At some point you will say something that lands badly: a casual comment, a misplaced joke, an assumption that seemed reasonable to you and wasn't to someone else. This is normal.

**When it happens:**
- Listen first, defend second (or not at all)
- "I didn't mean it that way" and "that still had that effect" are both true simultaneously
- A genuine apology doesn't require you to be a terrible person — just to acknowledge the impact

**What doesn't help:** extended guilt, defensive over-explanation, or making the other person manage your reaction to being corrected.

---

## Calling Others Out

If something bothers you, naming it is better than absorbing it silently. IUJ is small, and unaddressed friction tends to fester.

Short, direct, and non-dramatic works best: "That framing was a bit reductive, can I push back on it?"

---

## Language

**Names:** Learn to pronounce names correctly, or ask. Getting someone's name consistently wrong after being corrected is a form of dismissal.

**Assumptions based on appearance or accent:** Don't assume someone's country of origin, language, or expertise based on how they look or sound.
