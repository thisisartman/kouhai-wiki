IUJ has ~300 students from 40+ countries in a small campus in rural Japan. The conditions for building meaningful connections are unusually good — if you engage with them early.

---

## Why the First Two Weeks Are Different

Social groups at small graduate schools form fast and harden over time. The first two weeks of Fall term are when almost every connection that will define your IUJ experience is initiated. This isn't an exaggeration — the people you meet in orientation week are statistically more likely to become close friends than people you meet in month three.

**This doesn't mean you're locked in.** IUJ is small enough that you'll know everyone eventually. But it means early investment pays disproportionate returns.

---

## The IUJ Context

A few things are specific to IUJ:

- **Everyone is a stranger.** You arrive knowing nobody. So does everyone else. This is a leveller — social status from home means nothing here.
- **Diversity is the default.** Your cohort will include people from backgrounds you've never interacted with. The default mode is curiosity, not familiarity.
- **Proximity is intense.** You live, eat, study, and socialise in the same small space. Friendships deepen faster than usual — and tensions do too.
- **Seniors are accessible.** Second-year students have been through what you're going through. Most are genuinely willing to help.

---

## Practical Approaches

**Go to things.** Orientation events, dinners, informal hangouts — say yes to more than you think you want to, especially in the first two weeks. You can always scale back later. You cannot easily recover from early absence.

**Eat with people.** The cafeteria and dining area are natural mixing points. Don't eat alone by default.

**Introduce yourself repeatedly.** You will forget names; so will everyone else. Don't feel awkward re-introducing yourself in the first month.

**Seek across your comfort zone.** The natural gravity is to cluster with people from your country or language group. This is fine in moderation, but actively seek friendships across that boundary — the IUJ experience is significantly richer for it.

**Be interested.** The most effective social skill is genuine curiosity about other people's experiences. People from 40 countries have 40 genuinely different perspectives on almost everything — ask about them.

---

## When It Feels Hard

Not everyone finds socialising easy, and IUJ's intensity can make it harder, not easier. If you're finding it difficult:

- Lower the bar. A brief, genuine conversation counts. You don't need to be charming or interesting — just present.
- Find one person first. One real connection makes everything else easier.
- If social anxiety or introversion is a factor, the IUJ environment actually helps — structured events give you a reason to be somewhere without requiring you to initiate.

---

## Beyond the First Month

Once the initial formation period passes, friendships deepen through shared experiences — study groups, travel, late nights, difficult conversations, tough academic periods. IUJ compresses years of ordinary life into months.

The connections you build here tend to last. Alumni networks from IUJ are unusually tight — people who lived through the same experience in the same place maintain that bond.
