IUJ has its own event culture, and the Niigata region hosts some of Japan's best festivals. Both are worth knowing about and planning for.

## IUJ Campus Events

### Cultural Nights
Individual countries or regional groups host evenings showcasing food, performance, and culture. These are the signature social events of the IUJ calendar.

**Why they matter:**
- Best food variety you'll encounter on campus — multiple countries cook their own cuisine
- Genuine way to learn about places you've only read about
- Strong community-building function — hosting one creates bonds; attending others creates goodwill

**Typically:** Each event is organised by 1–5 students from a country/region; expect food, sometimes traditional dress, sometimes performance or presentation.

### Culti-Fiesta

IUJ's main annual cultural showcase, organised by the **GSO Executive Committee** each Fall term (typically November).

**Format:**
- Held in the **IUJ Gymnasium**
- Up to **16 groups** perform; slots are first-come, first-served
- Each performance is **5–7 minutes** (shorter acts welcome)
- Groups present their country, region, or community through music, dance, traditional dress, or any other format they choose
- MCs introduce each group — the GSO coordinates this with group representatives at a pre-event meeting
- You can be in multiple groups
- Sign-up deadline is typically ~1 week before the event; watch for the GSO announcement email

**Why it's worth going:** Students representing 60+ countries. The quality of performances varies wildly but the energy is consistently high. Best single evening for seeing IUJ's diversity in one place.

**Hosting:** If you want to represent your country or region, organise with other students and sign up early — slots go fast.

### International Festival (Open Day)

A separate, larger event from Culti-Fiesta — IUJ's public-facing festival, held in **late Spring (late May)**, organised jointly by OSS and the GSO Executive Committee.

**Format:**
- Open to the local public — roughly **2,000 local visitors** attend in a typical year
- **16 international food booths**, one per country/region group, first-come-first-served (requires a food booth application submitted to both OSS and GSO in advance, since the university needs food safety approval/licensing from the city office)
- **16 cultural stage performances**, 5–7 minutes each, same format as Culti-Fiesta
- An orientation session for participating teams is held ahead of the event

**Why it matters:** it's the one event where the surrounding Minamiuonuma community comes onto campus in large numbers — a different flavour from the internally-focused Culti-Fiesta.

### Sports Events and Tournaments
IUJ organises intra-campus sports events. Cricket, football (soccer), basketball, and badminton are common. These are often mixed-nationality, which makes them unusually good social opportunities.

## Local Niigata Festivals

### Naked Man Festival (Hadaka Matsuri) — Urasa

The most locally-specific festival on this list — it happens right in Urasa, and IUJ organises buses for it.

**When:** Early March (typically the first or second weekend)
**What:** A Shinto festival with roughly 1,200 years of history. A march departs from Okuni Jiman Kaikan at 18:00 and proceeds to Bishamon-do Temple near the river. Food vendors line the route.

**Getting there:** IUJ runs dedicated buses from the main entrance; the first bus is reserved for early sign-ups, the rest first-come-first-served. Return buses depart from the Urasa IUJ bus stop — check the return schedule carefully, since missing the last bus means arranging your own taxi back. Don't obstruct the march or participants, even for photos.

> 💡 Given the buses are run by IUJ itself and it's a few minutes from campus, this is one of the easiest local festivals to attend — arguably easier than the Nagaoka Fireworks below.

### Nagaoka Fireworks (長岡大花火大会)
**When:** Early August (typically first weekend)
**What:** One of Japan's three great fireworks festivals. The scale is exceptional — 20,000+ fireworks over two evenings along the Shinano River, including the famous Phoenix (フェニックス) display that spans 2km of sky.

**How to get there from IUJ:** Shinkansen to Nagaoka Station; ~30 min. Book well in advance — seats sell out. The crowds are massive; arrive early and expect the return journey to be slow.

> 💡 This is a mandatory IUJ experience. Clear your calendar for it.

### Tanabata Festival (七夕, *Tanabata*)
**When:** July (varies by location — some areas use August)
**What:** Festival celebrating the star-crossed lovers Orihime and Hikoboshi. Paper wishes (短冊, *tanzaku*) are hung on bamboo; streets are decorated with colourful streamers.

Look for local Tanabata events in Nagaoka and nearby towns.

### Yuki Matsuri (Snow Festival)
Niigata's winter festivals celebrate the snow rather than surviving it. Various events happen around the region in February — snow sculpting, illuminations, outdoor activities.

### Hanami (花見, Cherry Blossom Viewing)
**When:** Early to mid-April in Minami-Uonuma
**What:** The Japanese tradition of gathering under cherry trees as they bloom. One of the most reliably beautiful moments in the Japanese calendar.

IUJ's campus and the surrounding area have sakura trees. The timing in Niigata is later than Tokyo — late March to mid-April.

## Planning Around Festivals

- **Nagaoka Fireworks:** Book Shinkansen and accommodation months in advance if you plan to stay overnight. Even if going same-day, book the return train before you go.
- **Golden Week (late April–early May):** Japan's longest holiday period. Everything gets crowded and expensive. Plan travel early or stay local.
- **Obon (mid-August):** Similar to Golden Week for travel congestion.
