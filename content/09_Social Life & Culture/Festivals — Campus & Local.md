## IUJ Campus Events

### Cultural Nights
Individual countries or regional groups host evenings showcasing food, performance, and culture. These are the signature social events of the IUJ calendar.

**Why they matter:**
- Best food variety you'll encounter on campus, since multiple countries cook their own cuisine
- Genuine way to learn about places you've only read about
- Strong community-building function: hosting one creates bonds; attending others creates goodwill

**Typically:** Each event is organised by 1–5 students from a country/region; expect food, sometimes traditional dress, sometimes performance or presentation.

### Culti-Fiesta

IUJ's main annual cultural showcase, organised by the **GSO Executive Committee** each Fall term (typically November).

**Format:**
- Held in the **IUJ Gymnasium**
- Up to **16 groups** perform; slots are first-come, first-served
- Each performance is **5–7 minutes** (shorter acts welcome)
- Groups present their country, region, or community through music, dance, traditional dress, or any other format they choose
- MCs introduce each group; the GSO coordinates this with group representatives at a pre-event meeting
- You can be in multiple groups
- Sign-up deadline is typically ~1 week before the event; watch for the GSO announcement email

**Why it's worth going:** Students representing 60+ countries. The quality of performances varies wildly but the energy is consistently high. Best single evening for seeing IUJ's diversity in one place.

**Hosting:** If you want to represent your country or region, organise with other students and sign up early; slots go fast.

> 🔶 IUJ's own event pages also separately list an "**Around the World @ IUJ**" event (mid-November, "festival for the town introducing country games, handicrafts, dance, dress and music"). It's unclear whether this is the same event as Culti-Fiesta under a different/newer name, or a genuinely separate town-facing event. Worth confirming with GSO-EC before assuming either name is the current one.

### International Festival (Open Day)

A separate, larger event from Culti-Fiesta: IUJ's public-facing festival, held in **late Spring (late May)**, organised jointly by OSS and the GSO Executive Committee.

**Format:**
- Open to the local public, with roughly **2,000 local visitors** attending in a typical year
- **16 international food booths**, one per country/region group, first-come-first-served (requires a food booth application submitted to both OSS and GSO in advance, since the university needs food safety approval/licensing from the city office)
- **16 cultural stage performances**, 5–7 minutes each, same format as Culti-Fiesta
- An orientation session for participating teams is held ahead of the event

**Why it matters:** it's the one event where the surrounding Minamiuonuma community comes onto campus in large numbers, a different flavour from the internally-focused Culti-Fiesta.

### Community & Religious Celebrations

GSO-EC, on behalf of a specific community group within the student body, organises open-to-all celebrations for major cultural/religious occasions. Confirmed recurring examples from GSO-EC's own announcements:

- **Eid al-Fitr Celebration**, organised by IUJ's Muslim community, marking the end of Ramadan (date depends on the moon sighting each year, so it's confirmed only a day or two ahead). Held in the Gymnasium, free, open to everyone, no registration.
- **Africa Day**, organised by IUJ's African community, with music, dance, food, and games at the Gym/CNP and BBQ area.

Both are genuinely open events, not closed community gatherings — food and performance for anyone who shows up. Expect similar celebrations from other community groups on campus; these two are simply the ones with confirmed institutional backing.

### Grad Ball

An annual, student-only formal dance held in the IUJ Gymnasium near the end of Spring term (June), organised by GSO-EC as a send-off for graduating students, though open to the whole community, not just graduates. Each year has a theme and suggested colour palette (2026's was "Crimson Elegance," red/black/white) — dressing to theme is encouraged, not mandatory. In at least one recent year, GSO-EC ran a **Hall of Fame Awards** vote alongside it: students nominate classmates for superlative categories (Life of the Party, Most Friendly, Social Butterfly, etc.), with winners announced at the ball.

> 💡 Because it's student-only (no outside guests, family, or friends, due to venue capacity), don't plan on inviting anyone visiting from outside IUJ to this one.

### Graduation Ceremony

The formal degree-conferment ceremony, held **off-campus** at the Minami Uonuma City Hall (not on campus), typically in late June. IUJ live-streams it on YouTube for family who can't attend in person. Notable fixed elements per IUJ's own ceremony-day instructions:

- Buses run from campus to the venue and back, prioritising graduates and faculty; family and non-graduating students can use them only if seats remain
- GSO members, IM/IR Council members, and members of a Hult Prize-winning team get a specific on-stage commendation during the ceremony
- Graduates line up backstage in program order for the actual degree conferment, with marked positions on stage
- Back on campus afterward: a celebration party in the Gymnasium (for graduates, faculty, and VIP guests) and a **Tree Ceremony** at the Alumni Forest, where each graduating class places a class plaque — a small, low-key tradition worth knowing exists if you want to see it or participate as a class rep

### Other Recurring Campus Events

Per IUJ's own events calendar, a number of other recurring events happen alongside the bigger ones above: **Halloween Party**, **Valentine's Day Party** (GSO-EC), **Chinese New Year celebration**, an **International Snowball Fight** in Koide, a **Flea Market**, and **IUJ Olympics** (an inter-dorm/off-campus spring sports competition). Details on format/timing for each aren't confirmed here yet — treat this as a "these exist, watch for announcements" list rather than a full writeup.

### Sports Events and Tournaments
IUJ organises intra-campus sports events. Cricket, football (soccer), basketball, and badminton are common. These are often mixed-nationality, which makes them unusually good social opportunities.

## Local Niigata Festivals

### Naked Man Festival (Hadaka Matsuri), Urasa

The most locally-specific festival on this list: it happens right in Urasa, and IUJ organises buses for it.

**When:** The **1st Saturday of March**, confirmed consistently across IUJ's own event pages.
**What:** A Shinto festival with roughly 1,200 years of history. A march departs from Okuni Jiman Kaikan at 18:00 and proceeds to Bishamon-do Temple near the river. Food vendors line the route.

**Getting there:** IUJ runs dedicated buses from the main entrance; the first bus is reserved for early sign-ups, the rest first-come-first-served. Return buses depart from the Urasa IUJ bus stop. Check the return schedule carefully, since missing the last bus means arranging your own taxi back. Don't obstruct the march or participants, even for photos.

**Uniform (loincloth/sarashi):** If IUJ lends you the participation uniform (white pants and sarashi, the cloth wrap worn on the stomach), GSO-EC collects it back a few days after the festival — wash it first. Watch for the return-logistics email; it's a small detail but easy to miss once the festival excitement is over.

> 💡 Given the buses are run by IUJ itself and it's a few minutes from campus, this is one of the easiest local festivals to attend, arguably easier than the Nagaoka Fireworks below.

### Hakkaisan Fire Walking Festival

**When:** October 20.
**What:** A fire-walking ceremony at the Hakkaisan Shrine complex, roughly 3km from campus — IUJ's own guide presents this as a paired recommendation alongside the Naked Man Festival above, since both happen at the same shrine complex.

### Ojiya Events (near Urasa, per IUJ's own sightseeing guide)

- **Ojiya Balloon Festival**: 3rd weekend of February. Hot air balloons lift off from the snow field; you can even ride in one.
- **Ojiya Bull Fights**, between May and November, select weekends. Bulls "fight" sumo-style, led out by their trainers.
- **Ojiya Fireworks Display**: **September 8–9**. A distinctive format where individual fireworks are dedicated to specific people/occasions (births, birthdays, engagements, weddings); includes a Guinness World Record launch each year.
- **Echigo Tsumari Art Triennale**, a major art event held every 3 years (most recently 2018 per this source; confirm the current cycle before planning around it), drawing overseas visitors specifically for it. Runs August–early September.

### Nagaoka Fireworks (長岡大花火大会)
**When:** Early August (typically first weekend)
**What:** One of Japan's three great fireworks festivals. The scale is exceptional: 20,000+ fireworks over two evenings along the Shinano River, including the famous Phoenix (フェニックス) display that spans 2km of sky.

**How to get there from IUJ:** Shinkansen to Nagaoka Station; ~30 min. Book well in advance; seats sell out. The crowds are massive; arrive early and expect the return journey to be slow.

> 💡 This is a mandatory IUJ experience. Clear your calendar for it.

### Tanabata Festival (七夕, *Tanabata*)
**When:** July (varies by location; some areas use August)
**What:** Festival celebrating the star-crossed lovers Orihime and Hikoboshi. Paper wishes (短冊, *tanzaku*) are hung on bamboo; streets are decorated with colourful streamers.

Look for local Tanabata events in Nagaoka and nearby towns.

### Yuki Matsuri (Snow Festival)
Niigata's winter festivals celebrate the snow rather than surviving it. Various events happen around the region in February: snow sculpting, illuminations, outdoor activities.

- **IUJ Snow Sculpting Festival**: a campus event (not held every year), with snow sculptures and bonfires organised to promote intercultural exchange — distinct from the regional festivals below.
- **Muika-machi Snow Festival** (dates vary by year) and **Toka-machi Snow Festival**, which features the largest stage built entirely of snow in the world — both real, named regional events per IUJ's own event listings.

### Hanami (花見, Cherry Blossom Viewing)
**When:** Early to mid-April in Minami-Uonuma
**What:** The Japanese tradition of gathering under cherry trees as they bloom. One of the most reliably beautiful moments in the Japanese calendar.

IUJ's campus and the surrounding area have sakura trees. The timing in Niigata is later than Tokyo, late March to mid-April.

## Planning Around Festivals

- **Nagaoka Fireworks:** Book Shinkansen and accommodation months in advance if you plan to stay overnight. Even if going same-day, book the return train before you go.
- **Golden Week (late April–early May):** Japan's longest holiday period. Everything gets crowded and expensive. Plan travel early or stay local.
- **Obon (mid-August):** Similar to Golden Week for travel congestion.
